Main idea:

To induce ever increasing tension or to create an everlasting downfall, some composers use the Shepard tone. Shepard tone is an illusory audio effect that creates the sense the pitch of a sound is always ascending or descending.

The similar illusion in vision realm, is the barberpole illusion. The classic barberpole illusion includes a piece of paper with three printed red stripes on it. The rotation of the paper then creates the illusion that the stripes are continuously moving upwards or downwards. 

Later, Risset added a feature to the Shepard tone, which seems to glide continuously, and therefore is called Shepard-Risset glissando. The goal of this project is to implement this idea in Max/MSP.

Based on the work of Esqueda et al. [1], the effect is achieved using 10 harmonics that are one octave apart. A phasor object in Max/MSP is used that generates sawtooth waves with values ranging between 0 to 1. In order to create the sounds, the wave is multiplied by 120 to achieve the desired 10 octaves and then is added by 12 to reach the midi notes 12 to 132 (avoiding the octave “-1” of the midi notes). The result is then converted to frequencies and sent into cycle~ object as input to create the sinusoids. In parallel, to create a fade in when reaching the mid-range notes and a fade out when reaching the high-range notes, 0.5 is subtracted from the phasor result and the absolute value is calculated to reach a triangular wave ranging between 0 to 0.5. The result is then multiplied by -96. Minus to reverse the apex of the triangular wave to be in the middle instead of beginning and the end, and 96 since perceptually loudness of value of 48 decibels is audible and not hurtful. The result is changed into linear scale using dbtoa~ object, and multiplied by the result of the cycle~ object (fig.1)

Figure 1- An oscillator ranging 10 octaves multiplied by an amplitude envelope

 

This process is repeated 10 times, to achieve the desired 10 harmonics. To set the harmonics an octave apart a phase value is added to the phasor~ object from the right inlet. And, to send a bang to these phase values so that they are sent into the phasor~ object, dspstate~ object is used. Dspstate~ object, through its left outlet reports the status of dsp in Max/MSP (if it is on or off). In this way, every time the dsp is turned on, phase valued will be sent to the phasor~ as an input, setting the harmonics one octave apart (fig.2). The result of all of the harmonics are summed and sent to an ezdac~ object.

 

Figure 2. the ten harmonics are set an octave apart with inputting phase value to the phasor~ object

         

The frequency of the phasor~ set through its left inlet, determines how fast it sweeps from 0 to 1. Based on perceptual examination, the range of frequencies that does not break the illusion varies between ± 0.005 – 0.015. The negative frequencies create the illusion of ever descending pitch, while the positive frequencies induce the illusion of ever ascending pitch (fig.3).

Figure 3. Negative frequencies determine the descending pitch speed and positive frequencies the ascending pitch speed

 

The final result is shown in fig.4, where the kslider objects show the corresponding notes played on a keyboard for 5 of the harmonics.

Figure 4. Shepard-Risset glissando patch

 

Filters and Audio Effects:

The second part of the project involves implementation of a series of filters and audio effects.

         

General second-order filtering:

The first part of the patch, is a general second-order bandpass filtering. It uses filtergraph~ and biquad~ objects for different kinds of filters, the gain, cutoff frequency, and the Q of each filter can be changed using the graphic interface of the filtergraph~ object. Next to this part, through another filtergraph~ object and a cascade~ object a combination of filters can be implemented to meet the desired filtering effect. Number of filters and their properties can be assigned in the inspector mode of the filtergraph~ object, and then manipulated through its graphic interface. The example, includes a highpass, a lowpass and a resonant filter (fig.5).

Figure 5. General second-order filtering using filtergraph, biquad and cascade objects

 

Audio effects:

The audio effects include a delay line, a pitch shift, a flange, and a chorus.

The delay line takes input as the amount of delay for the right and the left channel, and how much the output signal is wanted to be affected by the audio effect (fig.6).

Figure 6. The delay line audio effect

The values are sent to the subpatch where the delay for each channel is implemented by a tapin~ and tapout~ object. The result is then multiplied by the value of how much the output is wanted wet. Then it is summed by the input signal and sent to its respective channel (fig.7).

 

Figure 7. Delay line subpatch receives the input and uses tapin-tapout objects to implement the desired delay

 

Next audio effect is the pitch shift [2]. It uses the theory of Doppler effect to do the pitch shifting. Doppler effect, classically experienced when an ambulance gets closer and then passes by you, is when the sound source moves relative to the listener. When the distance in between is decreasing, the perceived frequency increases and vice versa. To implement Doppler effect, a time varying delay line can be used.

          In the patch, there is only one input which shifts the pitch of the input signal to lower or higher (fig.8).

Figure 8. The pitch shifting audio effect

In fact, what manipulating the input does, is changing the frequency of the time varying signal of the delay line, where higher frequency leads to shifting the pitch to a lower frequency, and lower frequency shifts the pitch to a higher frequency. The subpatch shows how this is achieved (fig.9). Here to avoid the clicks introduced by the jumps of the phasor~ object from 1 to 0, the delayed signal is multiplied by a cosine which smoothly changes 1 to 0 to fade out the effect that the jump can have. Of course, now because of the multiplication of the signal by a cosine, its effect can be heard in the output as the sound oscillates with the frequency of the cosine. To avoid this, the signal is added with the result of a phasor~ that is 180 degrees out of phase with the first phasor~ time varying delay line. The result takes out the effect of the multiplication by a cosine.

Figure 9. The pitch shift subpatch implements Doppler effect using a time-varying delay line

 

Next audio effect is a flange. In flanging, a signal is added to its time-varying delayed version. The formula of which can be expressed as:

y[n] = x[n] + g x[n - M[n]]

where M[n] is the time-varying length of the delay line and g is the depth of the flange.

In the patch, the values that it takes in as input include the amount of the desired effect (wet/dry), the amount of delay, the frequency of the time-varying delay, and the depth of the flange (fig.10).

 

Figure 10. The flange audio effect

 

In the subpatch, a cycle~ object is used to generate the time-varying delay line. Its result is multiplied by the flange depth (variable g in the formula), and then multiplied and summed by the delay line. In this way the amount of the delay varies between 0 and two times the indicated delay time. This is sent to the tapout~ object and then the delayed signal is multiplied by the amount of desired level of effect and summed by the original signal (fig.11).

Figure 11. Flange effect implements a time-varying delay line multiplied by a depth factor

 

The last part of the patch, includes a chorus effect. The chorus effect is in many ways similar to the flange effect. It uses a time-varying delay line to alter the input signal and then sum it by the original signal. The time-varying delay is different in the way that is not wanted to have a predictable continuous change like a sinusoid, therefore rand~ object is used to generate the delay line. The patch receives inputs such as the amount of the desired effect (wet/dry), the amount of delay, the frequency of the time-varying delay, and the depth of the chorus effect (fig.12).

Figure 12. The chorus effect patch

In the subpatch, four delay lines are implemented, attempting to create a chorus of 4. The frequency and the depth received as input are multiplied by an arbitrary amount to generate different rate and depth for each delay line. Like the flange effect, the depth is multiplied by the time-varying delay line (here accomplished by rand~ object) and then multiplied and summed by the amount of delay time. The result is set as amount of delay into the tapout~ object. The delayed signal is then multiplied by the amount of desired effect and summed by the original signal. Two of four chorus effects are sent to each channel to distribute the chorus effect between channels (fig.13).

Figure 13. The chorus effect subpatch uses a rand~ object to generate the delay line
